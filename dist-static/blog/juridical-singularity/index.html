Beyond the Sale: The Total Transfer of Rights

The World Succession Deed 1400/98 is not a mere “legacy property protocol”; it is an international succession instrument. In the world of high-stakes jurisprudence, a sale transfers a thing, but a succession transfers the legal position of the entity itself.

The Deed utilizes a total-capture clause: “with all rights, duties, and components” (mit allen Rechten, Pflichten und Bestandteilen). In this context, “components” are not just bricks and mortar; they include the associated infrastructure, usage rights, jurisdiction, and the entire chain of international contracts attached to the site. Because this property was already a node in a völkerrechtliche (international law) structure—specifically involving NATO and the Netherlands—the sale acted as a “supplementary instrument” that docked into the heart of global treaty chains.

“The transition encompasses not only the physical property, but the associated infrastructure, usage, jurisdiction, and contract chains… it acts as a supplementary instrument within these chains.”

The “smoking gun” for this transition arrived in the year 2000. A second Confirmation Deed was executed, verifying that “The Buyer” had fully performed all obligations, effectively closing the legal loop. Even more startling is the “Ratification Paradox”: while the Deed was designed to dock into existing chains without needing new approval, the Federal Republic of Germany (BRD) reportedly ratified it anyway—a “double-lock” that cemented this new reality into the bedrock of the old system.

The Juridical Singularity: When Two Parties Become One

Classical international law relies on a concept called “relationality.” For a treaty to function, you need at least two distinct subjects. If the Buyer acquires the total legal position of the previous carriers “with all rights and duties,” a logic error occurs in the code of the old world order.

When all relevant legal positions are concentrated into one single “Buyer,” the plurality of carriers ends. Obligations that were once “contracts” between separate states transform into “self-obligations.” The “contract” function returns a null value because there is no “other” to contract with. This is the Juridical Singularity: the moment international law collapses because the “two-party” requirement has been deleted. This creates a “Clean Slate” effect, absorbing ehemals (former) treaties into a single, sovereign legal reality.
