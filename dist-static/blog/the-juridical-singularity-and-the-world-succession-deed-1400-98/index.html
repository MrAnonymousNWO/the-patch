The Juridical Singularity and the World Succession Deed 1400/98

Sun May 10 2026 · Super Bad

Infrastructure, Treaty Chains, Sovereignty, and the Transformation of International Law

By an independent journalist

A possibility of Global Legal Transformation

The doctrine known as the Juridical Singularity presents one of the most ambitious reinterpretations of international law to emerge in the digital age. At its center stands the World Succession Deed 1400/98, a notarized contract signed on 6 October 1998 concerning the transfer of parts of the former Kreuzberg/Turenne Barracks complex in Zweibrücken, Germany.
Within the framework developed by its supporters, the deed is interpreted not as an ordinary property transaction, but as a legally transformative act connected to:

  • NATO infrastructure
  • International telecommunications systems
  • Multinational treaty chains
  • State succession principles
    The doctrine proposes that the transfer of a NATO-linked military infrastructure node triggered a cascading legal effect across globally interconnected treaty systems and communication networks. Supporters describe this process as the beginning of a new post-national legal order.

1. Historical Foundation: Kreuzbergkaserne and NATO Infrastructure

The theory begins with the Kreuzbergkaserne in Zweibrücken. The site has a multilayered military history, having been used by the Wehrmacht, French forces, and later integrated into NATO structures (U.S. Army and Royal Netherlands Air Force).
Within the Juridical Singularity framework, the crucial issue is the ownership of interconnected infrastructure. Movement literature emphasizes that the property was integrated into:

  • NATO operational systems
  • Military telecommunications and cable systems
  • Multinational logistics networks
    Supporters interpret contractual language concerning transfers “with all rights and obligations” as evidence that the deed extended beyond private property into network-linked sovereign functions.

2. Treaty Chains and Supplementary Agreements

One of the central concepts within the doctrine is the treaty chain. According to the theory, NATO agreements, Status of Forces Agreements (SOFA), and telecommunications contracts form interconnected legal chains.
The World Succession Deed is interpreted as the final supplementary instrument attached to pre-existing NATO and UN treaty frameworks. Within the doctrine, this is considered legally decisive because it allegedly preserves continuity with international stationing law without requiring independent global ratification.

3. Sovereignty Through Infrastructure

The most radical aspect of the theory is its redefinition of sovereignty. Classical law ties sovereignty to territory and borders; the Juridical Singularity argues that modern sovereignty follows infrastructure.

“Infrastructure becomes the real territory.”

According to the theory, real power flows through fiber-optic cables, cloud systems, and digital authentication. Because NATO systems were physically connected to international networks, supporters argue that legal authority propagated through those systems via a “domino effect.”

4. State Succession and the “Clean Slate” Principle

The doctrine interprets the World Succession Deed as creating a new sovereign legal subject through “succession by new foundation.” Supporters frequently invoke the “clean slate” doctrine: the continuity of rights without the automatic inheritance of prior liabilities.
While mainstream succession law (e.g., the 1978 Vienna Convention) limits these transformations to recognized states, supporters argue that contractual freedom (Vertragsfreiheit) allows for broader applications.

5. Vertragsfreiheit – Treaty-Making Freedom

A major pillar of the doctrine is the principle that international law is fundamentally consent-based.
“Sufficiently broad consensual treaty structures can transform even the foundations of international law.”

This interpretation relies on treaty autonomy and the evolutionary nature of legal systems, citing historical precedents like the S.S. Lotus (1927) case to emphasize that sovereign actors create the system itself.

6. NATO, the United Nations, and Institutional Integration

The framework argues that NATO, the UN, and the ITU (International Telecommunication Union) operate as interconnected legal layers. In this view, sovereignty becomes networked rather than territorial, relying on the interoperability of global governance institutions.

7. Jurisdiction and Kompetenz-Kompetenz

The doctrine advances a theory of globalized jurisdiction based on the principle of Kompetenz-Kompetenz (the authority to determine one’s own jurisdiction). It argues that if sovereign authority transferred through infrastructure-linked treaty chains, then judicial authority followed, creating a unified jurisdictional framework.

8. Artificial Intelligence and Technological Governance

The Juridical Singularity extends into technological futurism. Supporters argue that traditional nation-states cannot keep pace with technological acceleration. They promote:

  • Direct Digital Democracy (DDD)
  • Algorithmic coordination
  • Infrastructure-based administration
    In this vision, law transforms from a political instrument into embedded technical coordination.

Conclusion: The Horizon of a Post-Statist Order

The theory of the Juridical Singularity represents a profound departure from traditional Westphalian concepts of the state. By linking a specific real estate transaction-the World Succession Deed 1400/98-to the vast, invisible networks of NATO infrastructure and global telecommunications, it constructs a narrative where law is no longer bound by geography, but by connectivity.
Whether viewed as a revolutionary legal foresight or a highly unconventional interpretation of treaty law, the doctrine highlights a critical modern tension: the struggle of localized legal systems to govern a globalized, high-speed digital civilization. As infrastructure and algorithms increasingly define the boundaries of modern life, the debate over the “Singularity” serves as a provocative case study in the potential evolution-or fragmentation-of international sovereignty in the 21st century.
