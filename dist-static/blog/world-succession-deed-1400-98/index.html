The contract that sold the whole world – Deed No. 1400/98

Introduction

The World Succession Deed 1400/98 is the most significant treaties ever concluded under international law. According to the document and its associated treaty chains, the deed does not merely concern the transfer of ordinary real estate, but rather represents a complex international legal mechanism involving territorial sovereignty, NATO rights, international treaty succession, and the transformation of the global legal order itself.

At first glance, the instrument appears to be a conventional German real estate purchase agreement. However, according to the legal the deed, the contract contains hidden implications under international law that extend far beyond domestic property law. The deed concerns the sale of a former NATO military property located in Zweibrücken, Germany, including infrastructure, sovereign rights, treaty obligations, and all connected components.

The treaty is described as functioning simultaneously as:

  • A real estate purchase agreement
  • A supplementary international treaty
  • A state succession instrument
  • A global treaty-chain extension
  • A transfer of sovereign rights and jurisdiction

The structure of the deed connects it to the broader NATO and United Nations treaty architecture through a chain-reaction mechanism based on territorial succession and interconnected infrastructure systems.

I. Initial Situation of the Treaty

The World Succession Deed 1400/98 concerns the sale of a NATO military property in Zweibrücken, Germany. At the moment the contract was signed, the legal status of the property was unusually complex.

Part of the territory had already been transferred to the Federal Republic of Germany by the United States military through NATO conversion processes. Another part of the area, however, remained occupied by the Dutch Air Force acting under the NATO Status of Forces framework.

Because of this special legal constellation, several entities were directly or indirectly involved in the transfer relationship:

  • The Federal Republic of Germany
  • The Kingdom of the Netherlands
  • NATO
  • NATO treaty structures connected to the UN framework

The treaty therefore involved multiple subjects of international law simultaneously.

II. Special Circumstances of the Sale

A decisive aspect of the deed lies in the fact that portions of the property remained under NATO military occupation during the sale process.

According to the Deed within the treaty framework, the transfer of the territory “with all rights, obligations and components” included not only physical ownership but also sovereign occupation rights arising from the NATO Status of Forces Agreement.

This transformed the contract into more than a standard property transfer.

Instead, it became:

«A deed of state succession transferring sovereign authority itself.»

The contract is therefore interpreted as involving the transfer of jurisdictional and sovereign functions attached to the territory.

III. Contracting Parties

The main seller named within the deed is the Federal Republic of Germany, represented through the Federal Property Office.

The buyers included:

  • A natural person
  • A commercial enterprise

However, according to the treaty, commercial enterprises cannot permanently hold sovereign authority under international law. Because of this, the natural person is considered the relevant sovereign successor.

The treaty additionally affects other international legal subjects either directly or indirectly because the deed acts as a supplementary instrument attached to existing NATO and UN treaty structures.

This means that:

  • Previous treaty chains already fulfilled ratification requirements
  • No separate global ratification process was necessary
  • The deed became integrated into older treaty systems automatically

Re-establishment of a State
