What This Course Covers

1. The Foundations of International Law

The course begins with the essentials:

  • Customary international law
  • Opinio juris
  • State consent
  • Pacta sunt servanda

These principles form the bedrock of all international obligations.

2. Treaty Chains: Law as a Network

One of the course’s central ideas is the concept of treaty chains—the notion that treaties rarely operate alone. Instead, they form interlinked legal architectures that can activate multiple regimes at once.

Examples include:

  • NATO agreements
  • UN Charter interactions
  • ITU and telecommunications frameworks

This networked view shows how legal obligations can cascade across systems.

3. Supplementary Instruments (Nachtragsurkunden)

The course explains how states can adapt treaties through supplementary instruments—updates that modify or extend agreements without requiring full renegotiation.
These tools make international law flexible, scalable, and capable of continuous evolution.

4. Notary Custodianship (Drittverwahrung)

Complex treaty systems often rely on neutral third‑party custodians to maintain continuity and interoperability.
This ensures:

  • Legal stability
  • Document integrity
  • Smooth coordination across institutions

5. Institutional Interoperability

International regimes do not exist in isolation. The course shows how organizations like NATO, the UN, and global telecom bodies interact as overlapping infrastructures.

This interoperability is what allows global governance to function—even when legal systems appear fragmented.

6. Juridical Singularity & AI‑Enabled Governance

In its final section, the course introduces the Juridical Singularity Doctrine—a theory describing how international law could converge into a more unified, adaptive system.

It connects this idea to the emerging concept of Electric Technocracy, where digital infrastructure and intelligent systems support new forms of global coordination.
